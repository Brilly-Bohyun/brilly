I am back from speaking at Open Source Summit + Embedded Linux North America 2026 in the United States.

This experience will stay with me for a long time. It was my first overseas technical talk in English, and it was also the first time I stood on the stage of a global open source conference after traveling all the way from Korea with the teammates I prepared with. It is difficult to summarize the experience only as "I gave a talk overseas" because there were so many things to think about before, during, and after the session.

On the way back, I had many different feelings at the same time: nervousness, gratitude, a few regrets, and a clear thought that I want to do better next time. More than anything, this trip made me think again about how I want to explain technical ideas, communicate with people, and grow inside the open source community.

## Preparing for My First Overseas English Talk

While preparing for the talk, the thing I cared about most was delivering the content accurately. Because I had to explain technical ideas in English, I focused on not losing the flow, not missing the core message, and building sentences clearly enough for the audience to follow.

![Welcome sign at Open Source Summit + Embedded Linux North America 2026](/brilly/assets/blog/ossummit-2026/photo-05.jpg)

_The venue itself made the conference feel real before the talk even started._

Compared to speaking in Korean, I had to think through many more details in advance. I had to separate expressions that felt natural to say from expressions that only looked fine on the slide. I also had to check whether the technical meaning still sounded clear after translating it into English. Preparing the slides was only one part of the work. I also had to prepare the exact way I could explain each idea on stage.

At first, I thought a good talk was mostly about delivering prepared content without mistakes. Since this was my first overseas English talk, that mindset felt natural. I wanted to say the right sentences, stay within time, and explain the technical details without leaving out anything important.

But after watching several sessions at the conference and seeing how speakers communicated with the audience, my standard for a good talk started to change. A technical talk is not completed only by reading a well-prepared script accurately. Eye contact, presence, pacing, and the ability to make the message reach people naturally are also important parts of the talk.

## Beyond Accurate Delivery

The biggest lesson I learned from this talk was that accurate delivery is important, but it is not enough by itself.

During preparation, I spent a lot of energy on not making mistakes in English, not missing technical points, and keeping the talk within the expected time. These things are important. In a technical talk, unclear language or inaccurate explanations can make it harder for the audience to understand the topic. Accuracy is still a basic responsibility.

However, the talks that stayed with me at the conference were not memorable only because the information was accurate. They had a natural rhythm with the audience. The speakers made difficult topics feel easier to follow, and they helped people stay with the story from beginning to end.

Brett Smith's session gave me a lot to reflect on. It helped me see how a technical talk can connect more naturally with people in the room. The speaker's technical understanding matters, but the way that understanding is communicated matters just as much.

This time, my main thought was, "I need to deliver what I prepared accurately." Next time, I want to think more about how to help the audience follow comfortably and think together with me. Understanding a technology well and communicating it well are connected, but they are not exactly the same skill. I want to keep improving both.

## The Realistic Difficulty of Communicating in English

The part that stayed with me almost as much as the talk itself was the regret I felt during networking.

There were so many people at the conference from different backgrounds. People were talking about Kubernetes, Cloud Native, Embedded Linux, open source communities, platform engineering, and many other areas. Even after sessions ended, technical conversations continued naturally in hallways, booths, and networking spaces. That felt like one of the real values of being there in person.

I wanted to talk with more people. I had questions I wanted to ask, and I also wanted to connect what I heard with the work I am currently doing. But because English is still not completely comfortable for me, there were moments when I could not be as active as I wanted to be.

I remember walking around the booths, wanting to start a conversation but hesitating. I had questions in my head, but turning them into spoken English in the moment was harder than I expected. A prepared talk allows you to practice your message in advance. Networking requires a much more immediate and natural kind of communication. I felt that difference very clearly.

That is why I was especially grateful to the people who opened conversations first. I was truly thankful to Lachlan Evenson, who kindly started a conversation with me. Knowing that he has been contributing to Kubernetes since its early days made the moment even more meaningful and inspiring. It was a short conversation, but meeting someone who has contributed to the open source community for such a long time gave me a lot of motivation.

This experience also made me look at my English skills more realistically. I need English not only for giving prepared talks, but also for sharing thoughts, asking questions, and building relationships in real time. If I want to participate more widely in global communities, communication skills are something I need to keep improving along with technical knowledge.

## Open Source Felt Like People, Not Only Code

This conference also reminded me that open source is not made only of code, projects, and talks.

Of course, code matters. Project direction, technical quality, documentation, issue management, and community operations all matter. But the energy I felt at the conference came strongly from people.

There were people who started conversations with newcomers, people who listened to others from different backgrounds, people who shared what they had learned, and people who explained similar problems from completely different perspectives. Seeing those moments in person made me feel more clearly how a community is built.

At a global conference, people with different cultures, languages, experiences, and roles gather in the same place. In that kind of space, it is important to create an atmosphere where people are not excluded and where people with different levels of experience can still join the conversation.

I hope I can become that kind of person for someone else someday. I want to be someone who can start a conversation first so that a newcomer feels less nervous, share the lessons I learned through trial and error, and contribute in small but meaningful ways so that more people can grow inside the open source community.

## A Journey We Prepared Together

This talk was not something I experienced alone.

I am truly grateful to TaejiKim and Woobin Hwang, who prepared the talk with me from Korea and traveled all the way to the United States together. Because we could review the session, go through the trip, and support each other on site, the whole experience felt much less overwhelming.

My first overseas English talk, the long flight, jet lag, an unfamiliar environment, and a big conference stage all came together at once. If I had gone through it alone, it would have felt much heavier. Because I had teammates with me, even the nervousness became part of a shared memory.

Preparing the talk was not only about making slides. We had to keep thinking about what message we wanted to deliver, how to structure the explanation, and what the audience might be curious about. Going through that process together taught me a lot, not only about speaking, but also about preparing as a team.

## Next Time, I Want to Do Better

The talk is over, but for me it feels more like the beginning of the next step than the end of one experience.

I learned a lot while preparing for the session, and I also came back with clear regrets from being on site. For speaking, I want to build a more natural flow with the audience. For networking, I want to become someone who can ask questions more actively and have deeper technical conversations.

This time, I was nervous and spent a lot of energy trying not to make mistakes in English. Next time, I want to go beyond accuracy and communicate more naturally. I want to be able to share technical perspectives more deeply and connect with people more confidently.

Instead of hesitating because English is still difficult, I want to keep learning, writing, and participating in wider communities. I want to become someone who can give better talks, explain technical ideas more clearly, and contribute meaningfully inside the open source community.

Since I traveled all the way to the United States for this experience, I think it is time to make fewer excuses and move a little more. I will use this experience as a reference point and slowly upgrade myself into global mode.
